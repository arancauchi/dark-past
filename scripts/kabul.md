TITLE: The Army That Vanished | Kabul 1842

---

COLD OPEN — GANDAMAK

At dawn on January 13th, 1842, seventy British soldiers formed a circle on a frozen hilltop.

They were surrounded. Their ammunition was gone. Frostbite had blackened their fingers.

Afghan voices called from the heights, demanding surrender.

One sergeant shouted back: "Not bloody likely!"

They fixed bayonets.

What followed was a massacre. Within minutes, most of them lay dead in the snow.

One week earlier, sixteen thousand people had fled Kabul under a promise of safe passage.

These seventy men were all that remained.

As always, viewer discretion is advised.

ACT 1 — THE TRAP

In 1838, Britain invaded Afghanistan.

The goal was simple: remove the current ruler and install a puppet king who would do as he was told. The army swept through the country, seized Kabul, and placed Shah Shuja on the throne.

It looked like victory. British officers settled in with their families, holding dinner parties and staging plays — a small slice of empire in the Afghan hills — surrounded by people who wanted them dead.

But the man commanding the British garrison was fatally unfit for the role.

Major-General William Elphinstone was old, sick, and indecisive. He ignored the growing unrest. When tensions finally boiled over, he did nothing.

---

ACT 2 — THE UPRISING

On November 2nd, 1841, a mob gathered outside the home of a British political officer named Alexander Burnes.

The night before, an enslaved woman had fled to his residence seeking protection. Her pursuers demanded she be returned. Burnes refused.

By morning, the crowd had grown. Stones flew. Muskets cracked.

The mob surged forward.

Burnes, his brother, and their household were dragged into the street and murdered. Their home was looted and burned.

Kabul erupted into open revolt.

Attacks spread across the city. The army's supply depot was stormed and looted. A British force marched out to retake the surrounding hills — and was driven back in confusion.

The roads to escape were blocked. Relief from the south was cut off by snow. Supplies dwindled.

The army was trapped.

---

ACT 3 — THE BETRAYAL

On December 23rd, the chief British envoy, Sir William Macnaghten, rode out to negotiate with the rebel leader, Akbar Khan.

It was an ambush.

Macnaghten was seized and shot. His body was dragged through the streets of Kabul and displayed in the bazaar — dismembered, impaled, a warning to all.

His hand was paraded through the city. His limbs were hung from meat hooks in the bazaar. For days, his remains were displayed as a trophy.

With their envoy dead and morale shattered, General Elphinstone accepted Akbar Khan's terms: the army would abandon Kabul and march ninety miles to Jalalabad — surrendering most of their weapons first.

Elphinstone agreed.

He had just condemned sixteen thousand people to freeze to death — unarmed.

---

ACT 4 — THE MARCH

On January 6th, 1842, the remains of the army trudged out of Kabul and began their climb into the mountains.

4,500 soldiers. 12,000 camp followers — women, children, servants. They carried what little they could.

Among them was a young officer's wife, clutching her infant to her chest. She would not survive the week.

The promise of safe passage was broken within hours.

Fire rained down from the heights. The rearguard faltered. Baggage animals bolted. Food spilled into the snow.

Akbar Khan sent word: hand over hostages — women, children, officers — and the attacks would stop.

Elphinstone agreed. The killing continued anyway.

On January 8th, the road narrowed to a choke point only a few metres wide. Cliffs above crawled with marksmen. The floor below was a jam of broken carts, fallen camels, and families stumbling through snowdrifts.

Muskets misfired in the damp. Powder ran low.

Officers shouted themselves hoarse trying to form ranks that no longer existed. Wounded were dragged a few yards, then left behind. Those who lit fires for the dying only painted targets for the marksmen above.

That day, over three thousand men, women, and children were shot or froze to death in the Khord-Kabul Pass.

By dawn the pass had become a trench of bodies — a road marked not by milestones but by corpses.

The survivors tried to march by night. But torches gave away their positions. The snow reflected every flicker of movement.

Afghan fire followed them even in the darkness.

Men stopped to rest and never stood again. Mothers collapsed in the snow, still holding their children. By morning, the living had to step over the dead just to keep moving.

Some soldiers, half-mad with cold, wandered off the road and were never seen again. Others sat down to die, too exhausted to care.

On the sixth day, barely a third of the way to safety, General Elphinstone surrendered himself in a final, desperate attempt to save what remained of the army.

It changed nothing. Only scattered parties stumbled forward.

---

ACT 5 — GANDAMAK

Which brings us back to that frozen hilltop.

Seventy survivors — the last remnants of the 44th Regiment — surrounded on a rise near the village of Gandamak. No way forward. No way back.

We can only imagine the terror they felt in those final moments.

A defensive ring formed in the snow. Redcoats and sepoys stood shoulder to shoulder, breath rising in pale clouds.

Their cartridges were almost gone. Many muskets too damp to fire.

Afghan voices called down, demanding surrender.

One sergeant shouted back: "Not bloody likely!"

They fired two ragged volleys. Then fixed bayonets.

What followed was brutal hand-to-hand combat. One by one, they fell.

The snow turned red with blood.

A handful were dragged away as captives. Most lay where they fell — red coats dark against the white.

---

THE SURVIVOR

Brydon had been slashed across the head, shot in the knee, and left for dead. He survived only because an Afghan fighter's sword broke on a magazine he'd stuffed in his hat.

He rode alone through the frozen passes, expecting death at every turn.

That afternoon, a sentry on the walls of Jalalabad caught movement on the road.

A single rider. Swaying in the saddle.

Dr. William Brydon came slowly into view — face crusted with blood, his pony stumbling with every step.

Guards ran to meet him. The animal's flanks were raw. The doctor's hands were blue on the reins.

"Where is the army?" someone called out.

Brydon looked up and whispered through cracked lips:

"I am the army."

For days, the garrison had lit signal fires on the walls, hoping to guide survivors home.

They kept the fires burning. No one else came.

---

OUTRO

Nothing could bring back the Army of the Indus.

Over sixteen thousand souls lost their lives in the frozen mountains of Afghanistan.

It remains one of the worst disasters in British imperial history.
